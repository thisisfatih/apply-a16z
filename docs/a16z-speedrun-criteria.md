## The speedrun program in brief

a16z speedrun awards up to $1M to early-stage founders building with AI. Applications are short-form. Decisions are fast. The bar is high: reviewers read hundreds of applications and move on in seconds if nothing stands out.

## What gets a human look

A reviewer pauses on an application when they see:
- A founder who clearly has earned access to this problem (not just interest in it)
- A specific, non-obvious insight about why this is the moment
- Evidence that something is already working, however small
- A crisp answer to "why us?" that is biographical, not aspirational

## The core questions reviewers are actually asking

### 1. Why does this founder know something others don't?
Not: why are they interested? Everyone is interested.
The answer must come from lived experience, domain immersion, or something they built and shipped that gave them a unique vantage point.

### 2. Why is now the right time?
Not: AI is a big trend. Every application says that.
A strong "why now" names a specific enabling factor: a model capability that just became available, a regulatory change, a distribution unlock, a cost threshold crossed.

### 3. What proof exists that this is working?
Not: "early traction" or "positive feedback." Specifics only.
- "12 paying customers at $500/mo, 0 churned"
- "4,200 waitlist signups, 23% week-over-week growth"
- "Pilot with [Named Company], processing $2M in transactions"

### 4. Is the market real, or real-sounding?
Not: "$50B TAM." Bottom-up: "10,000 mid-market logistics companies, each paying $30K/yr = $300M addressable."

### 5. Do the founders know why they will win?
Not: "passion and hustle." The answer must be an unfair advantage — network, domain depth, prior build, proprietary data, distribution.

## What causes immediate passes

- Generic problem descriptions that could apply to 50 companies
- Traction numbers without denominators (signups with no retention, revenue, or growth rate)
- "Why us?" answered with time-spent-in-industry rather than what-was-built
- No specific insight — the founder describes the problem space but reveals nothing they know that isn't public
- AI-generated-sounding answers (polished surface, no substance underneath)
- Team slides that emphasize credentials over output

## Language signals of weak applications

Weak: "We are building a platform to help companies leverage AI to streamline their workflows."
Strong: "We replace the 3-person vendor management team at mid-market logistics companies. First customer: [Company], saving $180K/yr. Signed in week 1 of the pilot."

Every answer should pass the "so what?" test. Read the draft. Ask "so what?" If you can't answer with a specific fact, the answer is weak.

## Application field weights (a16z priority order)

1. Team / Why us — 25%
2. What do you do / Problem / Solution — 25%
3. Traction — 20%
4. Business model — 15%
5. Why now / Competition — 15%

## The "Earned Secret" — The Core Test

**Official FAQ language:** "unique insights or 'earned secrets' that offer a distinct competitive advantage"

**Template:**
`[Who] discovered through [specific evidence] that [non-obvious truth]. This means [consequence]. Everyone else is [what competitors do] because [why they can't see it].`

**Contrarian test:** If a smart VC who covers this space would immediately agree with your insight, it's conventional wisdom — not an earned secret.

**Five questions to surface the earned secret:**
1. What do customers say the first time they see it work?
2. What assumption do competitors make that you think is wrong?
3. What surprised you in the first 10 customer conversations?
4. What do customers try to do manually before finding you?
5. What do analysts get wrong when they write about your space?
