# Answer Examples — High-Signal vs. Low-Signal
# For internal use by pitch-narrative and eval-answers skills
# All examples anonymized. Patterns, not prose to copy.

---

## Field: One-liner description

LOW (score: 3.1)
> "We are building an innovative platform that connects businesses with the resources they need to grow."

Problems: "innovative platform", "resources", "grow" — zero specificity. Could describe 10,000 companies.

HIGH (score: 9.2)
> "Automated compliance monitoring for community banks — we flag regulatory gaps in real-time before examiners do."

Why it works: specific buyer (community banks), specific job (compliance monitoring), specific outcome (flag gaps before examiners), implies both urgency and timing.

## Field: What insight do you have that others don't?

LOW (score: 2.4)
> "We believe the enterprise software market is ripe for disruption. Legacy players are slow to innovate and customers are looking for modern solutions."

Problems: "ripe for disruption", "slow to innovate", "modern solutions" — generic to every enterprise software pitch ever written.

HIGH (score: 9.5)
> "Community bank compliance officers don't fail audits because they're uninformed — they fail because the reporting cadence (monthly) doesn't match the risk cadence (daily). Every tool on the market was built for the reporting cycle, not the risk cycle. We discovered this after 40 interviews: every officer knew what was wrong, none had a tool that worked at the right frequency."

Why it works: specific mechanism (cadence mismatch), specific evidence (40 interviews, specific pattern across all of them), specific failure point in competitive products, implies why the founder can solve it (they asked the right question).

## Field: What traction do you have?

LOW (score: 3.8)
> "We have strong early traction with several beta users who are excited about the product and have given us great feedback. We are seeing strong engagement and users are coming back regularly."

Problems: no numbers, "several", "excited", "great feedback", "strong engagement" — all tell reviewers nothing.

HIGH (score: 8.7)
> "14 paying customers ($1,200 MRR), all sourced from two cold outreach campaigns in January. Average contract $85/month. Churn to date: 0. Three customers have expanded seats since signup. Our fastest deal closed in 4 hours from first contact."

Why it works: exact numbers, source of customers (cold outreach — shows GTM motion), churn data, expansion signal, velocity proof embedded in traction answer.

## Field: Founders

LOW (score: 2.9)
> "Jane Smith (CEO) has 10 years of experience in the financial services industry and is passionate about solving compliance problems. John Doe (CTO) is an experienced engineer who previously worked at several leading technology companies."

Problems: experience described as credential not proof, "passionate about", "leading technology companies" (which ones?), no zero-to-one proof.

HIGH (score: 9.1)
> "Maya Chen (CEO): ran compliance for a $400M community bank for 6 years — the target customer is her former self. Filed 200+ regulatory reports by hand; built the internal spreadsheet system half the bank still uses. Raj Patel (CTO): led infrastructure at [company], scaled their event pipeline from 10K to 40M events/day. Shipped the MVP of our current product in 11 days while Maya ran customer discovery."

Why it works: Maya IS the user (founder-market fit), specific prior zero-to-one (spreadsheet system still in use), Raj has relevant scale proof, timeline compression on MVP (11 days), roles are complementary and obvious.

## Field: What have you built so far?

LOW (score: 3.3)
> "We have built an MVP of our platform that includes the core features our users need. We are continuing to iterate based on feedback."

Problems: describes process not product, no specific features, no timeline, "core features" undefined.

HIGH (score: 8.9)
> "v0.1 (Dec 12): internal risk dashboard pulling from 3 core data sources — live with 2 banks. v0.2 (Jan 8): added automated SAR flagging, reduced manual review time from 45 min to 8 min per case (validated by 3 compliance officers). v0.3 (Jan 29): multi-user access + audit trail export. Currently: 14 paying accounts, all on v0.3. Next: FFIEC report auto-generation, scoped for Feb 28."

Why it works: version history with dates proves velocity, specific feature names, specific outcome metric with validation source, shows what comes next and when.

## Field: Why will you win against competitors?

LOW (score: 2.7)
> "Unlike our competitors, we are focused on the customer experience and ease of use. We have a modern interface and are agile enough to respond to customer needs quickly."

Problems: every startup says this, no named competitors, no structural reason.

HIGH (score: 8.4)
> "The two main players (Ncontracts and Quantivate) were built for bank holding companies — their data models assume a parent-subsidiary structure that community banks don't have. Adapting their product for a standalone community bank requires 6–9 months of implementation consulting. We built natively for standalone institutions: onboarding in 3 days, no consulting required. They can't rewrite their data model without breaking their existing customer base — we confirmed this with two former Ncontracts implementation consultants who now advise us."

Why it works: named competitors, specific architectural reason they can't copy (data model), specific customer pain they cause (6–9 month implementation), specific proof they're locked in (former consultants who confirmed), specific advantage (3-day onboarding).
